Digital surveillance tech facilitates ‘arbitrary’ border abuses
<p>The sharing of digital surveillance technologies by states looking to outsource their asylum and migration processes is contributing to human rights violations against people on the move, says a United Nations special rapporteur.</p> <p>Known as border “externalisation," Gehad Madi, the UN special rapporteur on the human rights of migrants, <a href="https://informaplc-my.sharepoint.com/personal/sebastian_klovigskelton_informa_com/Documents/Desktop/2026%20coverage/eVisa%2026">said</a> outsourcing these processes forms part of a “deterrence-based” approach to migration that focuses on restricting access to protections and reducing the ability of people to cross state borders.</p> <p>He added that externalisation measures, while often framed as forms of cooperation or partnership, should be distinguished from cooperation aimed at facilitating safe and regular migration.</p> <p>This is because, in practice, externalising states looking to shift their migration control functions and responsibilities onto third countries are actively seeking to either prevent or remove migrants, “practices that may be difficult to reconcile with the principle of good faith”.</p> <p>While there are many facets to externalisation – including political agreements between externalising and third countries that ensure extraterritorial processing of asylum claims, introduce prevention arrival frameworks, or expedite migrant removals – one key aspect of this process is the sharing of technologies and technical expertise.</p> <p>“To support these measures, externalising states provide financial assistance, training, equipment and broader capacity-building to migration and border authorities in third states,” said Madi, adding that such practices are often embedded into the wider cooperation frameworks agreed between states. &nbsp;</p> <p>“Increasingly, this includes the deployment of surveillance technologies, such as biometric systems, drones and border-monitoring tools, facilitating the tracking and interception of migrants.”</p> <p>The use of such digital technologies in the context of externalisation, Madi noted, may facilitate the violation of human rights in a number of ways.</p> <p>Highlighting the use of drones as an example, Madi said these can be deployed in ways that undermine a person’s right to leave a country or contribute to state refoulement practices (referring to the forcible return, expulsion, or rejection of migrants).</p> <p>On the collection of personal data by border monitoring or management systems, Madi added that these may give rise to privacy violations protected by the <a href="https://www.ohchr.org/en/instruments-mechanisms/instruments/international-covenant-civil-and-political-rights">International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights</a>.</p> <p>The export and deployment of various digital technologies, “in light of systemic discrimination embedded in migration governance” globally, also contribute to the creation and maintenance of racialised “mobility hierarchies” and discriminatory enforcement practices, resulting in the racial profiling or disproportionate targeting of migrants from the global South.</p> <p>Alongside state actors, Madi also noted the role of international organisations – including the UN, which helps run biometric surveillance systems and databases in refugee camps, such as those in <a externalisation measures or provide tools that enable authorities to enact them.</p> <p>Speaking with Computer Weekly in November 2024, refugee lawyer and author Petra Molnar said that third sector and non-governmental organisations (NGOs) are often involved in <a href="https://www.computerweekly.com/news/252492003/Humanitarian-data-collection-practices-put-migrants-at-risk">creating the “humanitarian” justifications for increasing border tech deployments</a>.</p> <p>Although people working in these organisations are often well-intentioned, Molnar said international organisations such as the UN, Unicef or the World Food Programme have “huge normative power” over the idea that “more data is better”, and are therefore&nbsp;<a href="https://www.computerweekly.com/news/252485852/Political-awareness-needed-to-ethically-handle-migration-data-panel-claims">a driving force behind normalising</a>&nbsp;a lot of the border tech enthusiastic provision of “intrusive surveillance powers”.</p> <p>A&nbsp;<a href="https://www.computerweekly.com/news/252525584/Surveillance-tech-firms-complicit-in-MENA-human-rights-abuses">report
