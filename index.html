<p>Andy Burnham has promised to deliver the "<a href="https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/live/cgrk4rpxnx7t">biggest rebalancing of power our country has seen</a>" as our new prime minister, with power taken away from Westminster and redistributed to all parts of the UK. This could herald the most significant act of devolution since the creation of the Scottish Parliament, Welsh Parliament, and Northern Ireland Assembly in the late 1990s.</p> <p>Although those changes helped bring power closer to people in some parts of the UK, they’ve also provoked endless debates about a so-called “postcode lottery”, where the quality, funding, and availability of services varies by geographic location, along with the seemingly eternal “West Lothian question” - where non-English constituency MPs still vote on England-only matters.</p> <p>Added to this is the growing phenomenon of regulatory friction - the compliance costs for businesses and citizens navigating different rules depending on where they happen to operate or live.</p> <p>Before rushing into another programme of major constitutional reform, the government should pause to assess where the current settlement is working well, where it falls short, and how it can be improved.</p> <p>This should include making time to consider the transformative impact of technology, a topic that remains surprisingly absent from devolution debates.</p> <p>Yet digital technologies and practices are a gamechanger in terms of improving how organisations work. They create new options for devolution that were simply unavailable when the current model was developed in the last century - options that can help to strengthen democratic accountability while reducing duplication and lowering costs.</p> <p>Rather than simply viewing devolution as a question of redistributing power, policymakers should take the opportunity to rethink the design of our
